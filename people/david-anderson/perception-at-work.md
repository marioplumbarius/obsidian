# Guide to Changing How People Perceive You at Work

## Summary
Dave Anderson (Scarlet Ink) argues that perception IS reality at work — your manager's
opinion of you isn't a fact, but it functions like one. You can't correct a perception
by arguing against it; you have to change what they observe. The article walks through
how this plays out for both high-ops and low-ops teams, where the same behavior earns
wildly different interpretations depending on the observer's frame.

## Decisions & insights
- **Perception ≠ fact, but it's treated like fact.** "I feel like your output is low"
  is an opinion, but denying it ("you're wrong!") does nothing. Only changing visible
  behavior can shift the interpretation.
- **You can't change the lens, only the input.** People filter events through their
  own value system. To change their conclusion, change the events — not the person's
  interpretive framework.
- **The Sherri case:** Same manager behavior (heroic fire-fighting) gets read as either
  "indispensable hero" or "hacker who never fixes root causes" — both are valid
  readings of the same data. The lesson: you need to actively manage which narrative
  is visible to leadership.
- **High-ops vs. low-ops perception:** A calm, low-ops team is seen as either
  "well-managed" or "an easy team / career resting place." The leader needs to make
  the *why* visible, or the wrong story wins.
- **Implication:** Don't just do the work — make the *right* interpretation of the
  work easy to reach for observers.

## Open question
What narratives could exist about my current work? Which am I reinforcing?
