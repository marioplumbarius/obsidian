# Kelsey Hightower on Generative AI

## Core stance

He's not anti-AI. He's anti-naive adoption.

> "I'm not a GenAI hater. I just don't like the naive promotion and adoption of it. I think it should be way more strategic. And since I think about GenAI as a tool versus the great human replacement, then I can use it in way more pragmatic ways."

The distinction matters: he engages with AI seriously precisely *because* he refuses to treat it as a religion.

---

## How he thinks about it

### People first, technology second

His whole career has been rooted in asking: how does this impact real people? He applied the same lens to crypto (currency resets hurt real retirees) and applies it here. When the industry celebrates AI replacing engineers, he immediately asks: what does this do to the people coming into the field for the first time?

### The "ban AI" advisory test

When startups pitch him for due diligence, he enforces a simple rule: **no one is allowed to say "AI" during the demo.** They have to describe the problem they are solving and the mechanism they are using — concretely.

- Good founders get *clearer* — they show a real problem and a real solution
- Weak pitches fall apart — "AI for healthcare" with nothing underneath

He then advises them to rewrite their website around the value they just demonstrated, not the umbrella term.

### Don't run to the ball

On the wave of AI pivots across startups:

> "If you all pivot to AI, you'll have a problem. It's like kids learning to play soccer — they all run to the ball. No strategy. Spread out, figure where you add value, and play your position."

His advisory work pushes companies to find their *specific* position in the landscape rather than chasing the hype center.

---

## On what AI is actually good for

### Closing the documentation loop

For decades, official docs gave hints and function signatures — not working examples. Developers chased Stack Overflow. AI tools close that loop: you get a full working example with context. He thinks this is a genuine improvement, and notes that AI is now *motivating* developers to write better documentation (to give agents context) in a way that decades of complaining never did.

### Intent-based APIs — we've been here before

He sees MCP and natural-language prompting as a rediscovery of something Kubernetes already did: wrapping imperative calls into intent-based interfaces. "I want a VM" instead of 7 sequential API calls. His take: this isn't a revolution — it's a reminder that most of our APIs were designed wrong from the start.

### The inference vs. caching problem

He's frustrated that people generate the same code blocks over and over with AI and call it fast:

> "If you find yourself generating the same blocks of code over and over — even though it feels fast or convenient — it is still insane."

The human loop has always been: write once → function → library → framework → baked into the OS. GenAI should follow the same loop, not replace it with infinite re-inference.

---

## On what AI won't replace

### Decision-making is the job

> "Software development is decision-making. Every keyword, every if statement, every function call is a decision we're making."

Writing code forces you to slow down. You discover mid-implementation that the data structure is wrong, the architecture needs rethinking, you shouldn't be collecting that field at all. If you offload too much to AI, you lose that deliberation — and that's where the real errors live.

### The zero token architecture

He posted this as a provocation: instead of burning tokens, *learn things and think for yourself*. The point isn't to avoid AI — it's to resist the reflex of outsourcing cognition before you've engaged with the problem.

### Depth still matters

Knowing how a compiler works, how memory management works — that's what lets someone *invent* a new programming language. If you only know the surface, you can't imagine what's below it, and you can't create anything genuinely new. He's not saying everyone needs that depth, but he's clear: if you want to build rather than consume, you have to go below the surface.

---

## On engineers' fear of being replaced

His framing is deliberately uncomfortable:

> "Software developers have been automating other industries away for decades. Don't be surprised if you find no sympathy from the professions you helped disrupt."

He asks engineers to do the reflection first — *why* am I afraid? Is it because my whole identity was "I'm the only one who can write code here"? If so, that was always a fragile position.

His actual advice: the engineers who are fine are the ones who understood the job was never just writing code — it was solving human problems with whatever tools exist. That job isn't going anywhere.
