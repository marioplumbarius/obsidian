# Working Backwards — The PR/FAQ

## Summary

The PR/FAQ (Press Release / Frequently Asked Questions) is Amazon's primary tool for vetting new ideas before any engineering, design, or budget work begins. You write a mock press release and FAQ as if the product already exists and is launching today. If you can't write a compelling one-pager, the idea probably isn't ready. Most of Amazon's major products since 2004 — AWS, Kindle, Prime Video — went through this process.

Source: [Working Backwards — PR/FAQ Process](https://workingbackwards.com/concepts/working-backwards-pr-faq-process/) · Popularized by Colin Bryar & Bill Carr in *[Working Backwards](https://www.theprfaq.com/r/working-backwards)* (2021)

---

## Origin

Jeff Bezos introduced the Working Backwards process at Amazon in the early 2000s. The core principle: start with the desired customer experience and reason backwards to what must be built, rather than starting with technology or capability and searching for a use case. It became Amazon's [first Leadership Principle](https://www.aboutamazon.com/news/workplace/an-insider-look-at-amazons-culture-and-processes) — Customer Obsession — made operational.

The PR/FAQ replaced PowerPoint decks and bullet-point proposals. Prose forces clarity that bullets hide. As Bezos put it: a well-structured narrative reveals whether someone actually understands the idea.

## The PR/FAQ Document

Total length: **≤ 6 pages** — one page for the press release, up to five pages for the FAQ.

### Part 1: The Press Release (~1 page)

Write as if it's the day the product launches and a journalist is covering it. Every word should make a potential customer lean forward.

| Section | What to write | Length |
|---|---|---|
| **Heading** | Product name + what it does, plainly | 1 line |
| **Subheading** | Who it's for and the core benefit they get | 1 sentence |
| **Problem** | The customer pain in concrete, specific terms — not abstract | 1 paragraph |
| **Solution** | How the product solves it; no jargon, no hedging | 1 paragraph |
| **Company quote** | A quote from a fictional senior leader stating *why* the company built this — sounds like a real person, not a press release | 1–2 sentences |
| **How it works** | Walk the customer journey step by step: first action, what happens next, outcome | 1 paragraph |
| **Customer quote** | A fictional customer quote with specific, tangible language about the impact on their life or work | 1–2 sentences |
| **Call to action** | Where to go, how to sign up, what happens next | 1 sentence |

> **Forcing function:** If the press release doesn't describe something meaningfully better — faster, cheaper, or a stepwise change in experience — than what already exists, the idea isn't worth building. ([ProductPlan](https://www.productplan.com/glossary/working-backward-amazon-method))

### Part 2: The FAQ (≤ 5 pages)

Split into two types of questions. The goal is to surface every objection before the meeting — not after.

#### External FAQ (customer perspective)

Questions the press and customers would actually ask:
- How does it work in detail?
- How much does it cost?
- What are the limitations?
- How is it different from [competitor]?
- What happens if something goes wrong?
- Is my data private/secure?

#### Internal FAQ (stakeholder/business perspective)

Questions finance, marketing, HR, legal, and executives would ask. This is where the hard thinking lives:

- **Market size:** For how many customers is this problem big enough that they'd pay to solve it? What's the TAM?
- **Metrics:** How will we know it's working? What are the key success metrics, and what are the targets at 1 year?
- **Feasibility:** What are the top technical or operational risks? What would cause this to fail?
- **Cost & headcount:** At minimum, T-shirt size estimates for revenue, cost to build, cost to operate, and team size needed
- **Dependencies:** What does this require from other teams? What are the blockers?
- **Competitive landscape:** What does Amazon/our company do if a competitor ships this first?

([Working Backwards — PR/FAQ Process](https://workingbackwards.com/concepts/working-backwards-pr-faq-process/))

## The Writing Process

The PR/FAQ is **iterative by design**. Early drafts have gaps, unanswered questions, and weak reasoning — that's expected and the point. ([Commoncog](https://commoncog.com/putting-amazons-pr-faq-to-practice/))

1. **First draft**: Write alone. Get the customer story on paper. Expect it to be rough.
2. **Circulate early**: Share with a small group before it's polished. Holes in reasoning surface faster with readers than with more writing.
3. **Debate and revise**: Each review cycle improves the clarity of the *idea*, not just the prose. If reviewers keep asking the same question, that's a gap in the product thinking, not the writing.
4. **Stop when it's read-worthy**: The document is ready when a skeptical senior leader can read it and clearly understand what's being proposed, why, for whom, and at what cost.

The meeting where the PR/FAQ is reviewed is typically a silent read: everyone reads the document at the start of the meeting, then discussion begins. ([Amazon's writing culture](https://www.theprfaq.com/articles/amazon-writing-culture))

## Key Principles

**Start with the customer, not the technology.**
You are not writing a capabilities document. You are writing a customer story. The product exists in the document before it exists in reality. ([Ian McAllister, ex-Amazon Director](https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/applying-amazons-working-backwards-process-leaders-ian-mcallister))

**If the press release is boring, the product idea is weak.**
A vague or uninspiring press release is signal. It means the team hasn't yet found what's genuinely valuable. Don't paper over it with more meetings — rewrite.

**Write at customer level, not internal level.**
Avoid internal names, technical infrastructure, org chart references, team jargon. If the customer wouldn't care about it, it doesn't belong in the press release.

**No hedging.**
"We think customers might..." or "potentially could..." are red flags. Either the product solves a real problem or it doesn't. Write it like it does. If you can't, that's the feedback.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Product for everyone** → Great products are built for a *specific* customer segment with a *specific* problem. "Any business" or "all consumers" is a weak customer definition. ([Medium — intrico.io](https://intrico.io/blog/strategy-tool-amazons-prfaq))
- **Polishing prose instead of thinking** → Teams spend cycles on word choice when the real problem is they haven't solved the hard product question yet. Polish is a distraction from reasoning.
- **Treating it as a one-time artifact** → The best PR/FAQs evolve over months as the team learns. Revisit and revise as assumptions are tested.
- **Skipping the internal FAQ** → The external FAQ is easy; the internal FAQ is where the real accountability lives. Skipping it means shipping a product without thinking through cost, feasibility, or success metrics.
- **Company quote that sounds like a company quote** → If the quote could appear in a generic press release from any company, rewrite it. It should sound like a specific human being who cares.

## When to Use It

- Any significant new product or feature initiative
- A new business line or market entry
- A major internal tool or operational process change
- Evaluating whether an idea is worth any investment at all

Not needed for small bug fixes, incremental improvements, or work that is clearly within scope of an existing approved initiative. The PR/FAQ is calibrated to ideas where there is genuine uncertainty about whether to build — and genuine risk if the wrong thing gets built.
